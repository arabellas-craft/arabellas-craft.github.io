The Four Treasures of the Tuatha Dé tarot spread reads a situation through the four sacred objects the Tuatha Dé Danann carried into Ireland: the Stone of Destiny, the Spear of Lugh, the Sword of Nuada, and the Cauldron of the Dagda. Four cards: what's true, where you're aiming, what needs cutting, and what you already have with you.

## The Treasures in Irish Tradition

The medieval *Lebor Gabála Érenn* (Book of Invasions) tells how the Tuatha Dé arrived in Ireland from "the northern islands of the world," bringing one treasure from each of four cities. *Cath Maige Tuired*, the Battle of Moytura, and the related lore describe what each one did:

- **Stone of Destiny** from Falias: the stone that cried out under the rightful king at Tara
- **Spear of Lugh** from Gorias: no battle could be sustained against the one who held it
- **Sword of Nuada** from Findias: once drawn, no enemy escaped
- **Cauldron of the Dagda** from Murias: no company ever left it unsatisfied

For the bigger mythological context, see [the Mythological Cycle overview](/irish-mythology-mythological-cycle).

Each treasure does something specific. The Stone of Destiny tells truth. The Spear tests aim. The Sword shows what needs to be released. The Cauldron reveals sufficiency. Read together, they cover four registers a situation can be evaluated against: what's real, where you're going, what's costing you to hold, and what's already enough.

## When to Use This Spread

Use this spread when a situation has gotten complex and you can't tell which question to ask first.

It works well when:

- You're trying to make a decision but keep cycling through the same considerations without resolving anything
- A project or relationship has multiple things going on at once and you need a way to sort them
- You suspect your aim has drifted from your original intent and want to test it
- You're working in a Celtic Reconstructionist or Irish polytheist framework

## The Card Positions

**Card 1: The Stone of Destiny — What is genuinely true about this situation?**

The Stone of Destiny cried out beneath the rightful king. It tested legitimacy by responding to truth. This card does the same kind of work: it names what's actually true about your situation, not what you've been telling yourself or hoping it would be.

The Hierophant here might point to a structure or commitment that's holding more weight than you've acknowledged. The Moon names something you've been pretending is clear when it isn't. The Eight of Pentacles identifies real, ongoing work that's the actual answer to a question you've been asking abstractly.

**Card 2: The Spear of Lugh — Where am I aiming, and is the aim true?**

Lugh's spear was a weapon of perfect aim, won by his many skills. This card asks where your attention is currently pointed and whether that aim is serving the situation.

Aim has two parts: direction and intention. You can be aiming somewhere consciously while your effort goes somewhere else entirely. The Spear position surfaces that gap.

The Three of Wands here points to a goal you've been planning toward that's still worth pursuing. The Seven of Cups names too many possible targets, none of them committed to. The Knight of Swords identifies an aim that's clear but possibly too aggressive for what the situation can hold.

**Card 3: The Sword of Nuada — What needs to be cut?**

Nuada's sword was inescapable once drawn. This card names what you need to release, end, or actively remove from the situation.

The thing being cut isn't always negative. Sometimes it's a previous version of a goal that no longer fits. Sometimes it's a relationship that was right once but isn't now. Sometimes it's a story you've been telling that protected you in an earlier chapter but now keeps you stuck.

The Eight of Cups points to walking away from something you've outgrown. The Tower identifies a structure already breaking and asks you to let it fall. The Five of Pentacles names a scarcity story that's no longer true and needs to be set down.

**Card 4: The Cauldron of the Dagda — Where is abundance already present?**

Anyone who ate form the Dagda's cauldron always left satisfied. This card names what's already sufficient in your situation. Sometimes the most useful information is what's working and what you can rely on while the rest of the picture moves.

The Ten of Pentacles here points to material or familial stability that's genuinely in place. The Empress names creative or relational fertility you've stopped counting because it became background. The Star identifies a steady source of meaning you've been treating as a given.

## Reading the Spread

Lay all four cards before you read any of them.

Read the Stone of Destiny first, no matter how much you want to skip to the Sword or the Cauldron. The Stone of Destiny sets the frame. Every other card in the reading depends on what it shows. Without an honest reading of Card 1, the other three positions are answering questions about a situation you've imagined rather than the one you're actually in.

Then read Cards 2 and 3 together. The Spear (where you're aiming) and the Sword (what needs cutting) are in conversation. Often what needs to be cut is whatever's blocking the aim. Sometimes it's the aim itself.

Read the Cauldron last. It's the counterweight to everything else in the spread. After you've named what's true, where you're going, and what has to go, the Cauldron asks you to also see what's already enough.

Look at the suits across all four positions. Three or more cards from one suit tells you what register the situation is being decided in. All Pentacles points to a material or practical situation. All Cups names emotional content that hasn't been fully reckoned with. All Swords surfaces a conflict about clarity, language, or truth-telling. All Wands points to action and momentum.

The Major Arcana count matters here too. Two or more Majors signals a turning point with more weight than ordinary maintenance can address.

## Tips for Beginners

You don't need to know the mythology in detail to use this spread. The position questions stand on their own. If you want the deeper context though, [the Mythological Cycle overview](/irish-mythology-mythological-cycle) covers the gods and stories the treasures come from, and [the Celtic Reconstructionism cosmology article](/celtic-reconstructionism-theology-cosmology) covers the broader framework these objects sit inside.

The Sword position (Card 3) is often the hardest to accept. People want to argue with it. If you find yourself building a case for why the card doesn't apply, write that case down and read it back to yourself. The defense usually contains the answer.

Don't shortcut the Cauldron. If the spread points to abundance you'd rather dismiss as "not enough," sit with that. The position is asking you to recalibrate what counts.

You don't need a Celtic-themed deck. Any deck works. A worn deck, a deck someone gave you, a deck of regular playing cards (here's [how to read those](https://ko-fi.com/s/95f9c29692)). The spread maps onto whatever deck you have.

Write the reading down. The Four Treasures spread tends to surface things that come into focus over a few weeks. The Sword in particular often makes more sense once you've started doing the cutting it asked for.

This spread also pairs well with seasonal Irish readings, especially around [Bealtaine](/free-tarot-spreads/bealtaine-tarot-spread) and Samhain, when the question of what to carry forward and what to leave behind is already being asked at a wider scale. If you want a related Celtic spread with a different shape, the [Three Realms (Land, Sea, Sky) tarot spread](/free-tarot-spreads/three-realms-celtic-tarot-spread) works from the same cosmology but covers the situation across realms rather than treasures.
