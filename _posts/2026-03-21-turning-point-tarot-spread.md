The Turning Point Tarot Spread is for moments when something is already shifting and you need to get clear on what that means. Three cards: what to release, what to welcome in, what to hold onto. No ritual prep required, no special deck, no prior experience.

## When to Use This Spread

Use this spread when you're in the middle of something changing.

That could be a major life transition: a move, the end of a relationship, a career change. It could also be quieter — a season turning over, a habit you've been reconsidering, a friendship that's shifted.

The spread works best when you can already feel the turning point. You don't need certainty about what comes next, just enough self-awareness to look honestly at where you are.

Skip it when you want a yes/no answer on a specific decision. This spread won't tell you whether to take the job or leave the city (in fact [tarot isn't very good at yes/no questions](/yes-no-tarot)). It'll help you see what you're bringing to that decision, what you're ready to let go of, and what still matters to you.

## The Card Positions

**Card 1: What to Release — What am I holding out of habit rather than intention?**

This card names what you've been carrying past its usefulness. The key phrase is "out of habit rather than intention" — there's a difference between choosing to keep something and keeping it because you haven't examined it.

This position often surfaces things people don't expect. Not the obvious burden, but the quieter one: an identity you've been clinging to, a story you keep telling about yourself, a way of protecting yourself that was necessary once but isn't now.

The question is specific: *habit* rather than intention. If you'd consciously choose to keep this, it doesn't belong in this position. If you're keeping it because you haven't stopped to question it, that's your answer.

**Card 2: What to Welcome In — What am I ready to let in?**

This card shows what you're genuinely prepared to receive. Not what you want or hope for, but what you're actually ready for given where you are right now.

Readiness is doing real work here. This card is about what's possible in your current state, not what's theoretically desirable.

Pay attention to your first reaction to this card. Relief means you already knew. Resistance means you're not quite as ready as you thought. Both responses tell you something.

**Card 3: What to Hold Onto — What should I choose to keep?**

This card is the counterweight. Transitions create pressure to shed everything and treat the turning point as a blank slate. This position pushes back.

Not everything from before needs to go. Some things are worth carrying forward deliberately. This card identifies what still has value and belongs in the next chapter.

The word "choose" in this position matters. This isn't what you can't avoid bringing with you — it's what you're actively selecting.

## Reading the Spread

Lay all three cards before you read any of them individually.

Look at the suits first. Three cups means the transition is primarily emotional — relationships, feelings, and self-connection are at the center. Three swords means conflict or the need for clarity. Three wands points to movement and energy. Three pentacles puts the focus on material concerns: security, work, physical health.

Then look at whether Card 1 and Card 3 tell a coherent story. What you release and what you keep should have a logical relationship. If Card 1 is the Six of Cups (nostalgia, the past) and Card 3 is the Ace of Wands (new energy), the spread is telling you that honoring the past and building momentum aren't in conflict — they're just sequential steps.

Card 2 sits between them. Read it last, after you've understood what's being released and what's being kept. What you're ready to welcome only makes sense in relation to those two.

## Tips for Beginners

You don't need to interpret every possible meaning of a card. Pick one thing it's saying to you and work with that.

If a card confuses you, describe it out loud or on paper — the image, the figures, the atmosphere. Your own description almost always contains the interpretation.

Write down all three cards and your notes immediately. Memory rewrites itself quickly during transitional periods. What's obvious today will seem unclear in two weeks.

You don't need a specific deck. A worn deck works. A deck you're not fond of works. The spread doesn't require a deck that "matches the energy" of the moment. It just requires a deck.

## A Note on High-Stakes Readings

Turning points make tarot feel high-stakes. When things genuinely matter, the pressure to get the reading "right" goes up.

This spread isn't predicting what happens next. It's helping you name what you already know: what's been draining you, what you're actually prepared for, what's worth protecting. The cards give you a structure for saying those things out loud.
