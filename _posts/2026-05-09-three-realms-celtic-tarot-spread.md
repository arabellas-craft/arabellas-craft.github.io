The Three Realms Tarot Spread reads land, sea, and sky as three sources of information on a single situation. Three cards: what's solid and present, what's moving emotionally below the surface, and what's shaping things from above your reach. The spread is grounded in Celtic cosmology, specifically the Old Irish triad of *nem*, *talam*, and *muir*, rather than the four-element framework borrowed from nineteenth-century ceremonial magic.

## Where the Three Realms Come From

The Celts didn't organize the world around four elements and four directions. That structure came into modern paganism through the Hermetic Order of the Golden Dawn in the late 1800s, and entered Wicca from there.

Pre-Christian Irish cosmology used a different map. Three realms, one for each domain of the visible world:

*Nem* (niv): sky
*Talam* (TAH-lum): land
*Muir* (mwir): sea

Swearing by sky, land, and sea was a standard way of invoking the whole of existence in the Irish tradition. Erynn Rowan Laurie puts it directly in *[A Circle of Stones](https://bookshop.org/a/84772/9781536558241)*: "All things lived within the circle." For a longer treatment of how this cosmology fits inside Celtic Reconstructionism, see [my CR cosmology article](/celtic-reconstructionism-theology-cosmology).

The three realms aren't symbolic stand-ins for elemental qualities. They name the actual structure of the world as Irish tradition understood it. That makes them a sturdy frame for divination. Each realm asks a different kind of question, and together they give you a picture of a situation across three honest registers.

## When to Use This Spread

Use this spread when a situation has more than one layer and you can't quite see all of them at once.

It works well when:

- A practical decision feels weighed down by emotional content you can't pin to anything specific
- Something is going on beneath the surface and you want to name it before it surfaces on its own
- You suspect a wider pattern is influencing things and want to test that hunch
- You're working in a Celtic Reconstructionist or Irish folk magic framework

Skip it when you want a yes/no answer or a single piece of advice (tarot [isn't great at yes/no questions](/yes-no-tarot) regardless of spread). The Three Realms isn't built to give you a decision. It shows you the full shape of what you're dealing with.

## The Card Positions

**Card 1: Talam (Land) — What's solid, rooted, and present in this situation.**

Land names the part of the situation you can stand on. The facts you already have, the resources you can count on, the actual circumstances rather than the story you're telling about them.

This card pulls you back to what's real. If the rest of your week has been spent in worry or anticipation, the Land position grounds the reading in what's actually present. What's already in place. What's not going anywhere.


**Card 2: Muir (Sea) — What's emotional, fluid, or moving below the surface.**

Sea names what's moving and hard to see. Emotional currents, intuitions you haven't put into words, the half-formed impulses that shape decisions before reason catches up.

In Irish cosmology, the sea is also a boundary with the Otherworld. *Tír fo Thuinn*, the Land Under the Wave, is one of the names for the Otherworld. So the Muir position also asks what's coming up from a place you can't fully see: an old grief surfacing, an ancestral pattern you've been carrying without naming, a connection that's influencing your reactions without your conscious permission.


**Card 3: Nem (Sky) — The higher pattern, the influence beyond your immediate reach.**

Sky names the wider context you're sitting inside. The cultural moment, the season, the patterns that are bigger than your individual choices but that shape what those choices mean.

This card is  the frame around the situation. The external pressures, the timing, the things in motion that don't care about your preferences.

Sky cards are useful because they let you stop blaming yourself for things you didn't cause. Some patterns aren't yours. You're working inside them.

## Reading the Spread

Lay all three cards before you read any of them.

Look at them together first. Land, sea, and sky are meant to be in relationship. Solid Pentacles in Card 1 with stormy Cups in Card 2 tells you the practical situation is more stable than your emotional state is registering. Wands in Card 3 over watery cards below points to outside momentum pushing on something that's still unsettled inside you.

Then read each card on its own, in order. Land first: what's actually true. Sea second: what's moving underneath. Sky last: what's shaping all of it from outside.

Once you've read each card, look at the suits and Major Arcana count across the three positions. Three Major Arcana points to a moment with real weight; the situation is bigger than ordinary. All three Minors suggests something everyday, working itself out at human scale.

If a card feels out of place (a watery card in the Land position, an earthy card in the Sky position), read that as the spread telling you something. Cups grounding the Land position can mean your emotional life has become the most stable part of your situation. Pentacles in the Sky position can name material conditions that have grown into a larger frame than they used to be.

## Tips for Beginners

Read what the card actually shows, not what you wish it would say. The Three Realms spread tells you the shape of the situation. It doesn't tell you the resolution.

You don't need a Celtic-themed deck. Any deck works. A worn deck, a deck someone gave you, even a deck of regular playing cards (here's [how to read those](https://ko-fi.com/s/95f9c29692)). The spread doesn't require visual matching to the cosmology.

Write the reading down. Three Realms readings tend to come into focus weeks later, when one of the three positions turns out to have been more important than you thought at the time.

If you want more context on the three realms framework before you sit with a deck, [the CR cosmology article](/celtic-reconstructionism-theology-cosmology) walks through the primary sources in detail. If you want a related Irish-cosmology divination tool that doesn't use tarot, [Ogham divination](/how-to-read-ogham-divination) draws from the same tradition.

This spread sits well alongside seasonal readings tied to liminal Irish thresholds, especially [Bealtaine](/free-tarot-spreads/bealtaine-tarot-spread) and Samhain, when the sea-realm card tends to carry more weight than usual.
