Well into the twentieth century, on the first day of autumn in Ireland, people climbed the nearest hill, or gathered at a lake or river, and met the neighbouring parishes there for a day of food, music, and the first ripe berries.[^1]

Lúnasa fell on 1 August, but the gathering usually moved to the nearest Sunday: the last Sunday in July in most places, the first Sunday in August in others.[^1] That's why the day carries so many Sunday names. Garland Sunday, Bilberry Sunday, Reek Sunday, and Crom Dubh's Sunday all mark the same turn in the year, each named for what people did or where they went.

This piece is about the customs themselves, recorded across Ireland into the twentieth century. For the plain overview, see [what is Lughnasadh?](/what-is-lughnasadh); the festival's [pre-Christian origins](/lunasa-pre-christian-origins) and the figure of [Crom Dubh](/crom-dubh-lughnasadh) each have their own article.

## The gathering on a height

The heart of the day in most districts was the trip out to a traditional spot, usually a hilltop or a place beside a lake or river, where crowds from the surrounding parishes came together.[^1] The day went on food and drink, music and dancing, and sports and trials of strength among the young men. Courting and matchmaking were a recognized part of it.[^1]

The climb was part of the appeal. Rough ground kept the very old and the very young at home, so the hill belonged to the young and able for the day.[^1] MacNeill, as reported by Danaher, counted 78 of these assembly hills across the country.[^1]

In the Slieve Bloom the gathering was called Height Sunday, with games and young men racing round and leaping the evening fires.[^1] In Camross, Co. Laois, people went up on "Heights Sunday" to gather heather on the hill and make besoms from it.[^2]

## Bilberries on the hill

The first fruit of the day was the bilberry, and the day picked up berry names to match: in Inishowen it was Heatherberry Sunday.[^1] The berry goes by several names in Ireland, fraughan and whortleberry among them, but it's all the same small dark berry that ripens on the hills in late summer. People ate them where they picked them, and at home mashed them with fresh cream and sugar to finish the festive meal.[^1]

On the Little Sugarloaf in Co. Wicklow, people gathered for the day at a spot still known as the Fraughan Bushes.[^3]

## The first-fruits meal, and the rule against cutting early

The day marked the first eating of the new harvest. For the last two centuries that meant new potatoes, dressed with whatever the household could afford, from cabbage or fish to bacon or fresh meat.[^1] An older version of the custom used the new corn instead, reaped that morning and made into bread or porridge by evening.[^1]

The other side of the first-fruits rule was a ban on jumping the gun. Cutting corn or digging potatoes before the festival was thought improper, a sign of bad husbandry; the old potatoes, meanwhile, were said to turn unwholesome once Garland Sunday had passed, fit only for the pigs.[^1] The date told you when the harvest could begin.

## Patterns and pilgrimages

Lúnasa was a favourite time for "patterns," the devotions held at a holy well or a saint's shrine: making the rounds, drinking or bathing in the water, leaving a small token, and then joining the fun of the day.[^1] MacNeill, as reported by Danaher, listed 80 wells visited at this season, though not all of them tie to the harvest festival.[^1]

The great survivor is Croagh Patrick, the Reek, climbed on the last Sunday in July on what people call Reek Sunday. The pilgrimage is over a thousand years old; a papal letter of 1432 already mentions it.[^1] The [origins article](/lunasa-pre-christian-origins) covers why the mountain was a gathering-place long before Patrick's name was attached to it. Three lesser mountain pilgrimages of the same season have since faded: Mount Brandon in Kerry, Slieve Donard in Down, and Church Mountain in Wicklow.[^1]

The folk record keeps both kinds. On Mount Callan in Co. Clare people went up for a pattern on "Lammas Sunday,"[^4] and at Lough Keeran in Co. Mayo a well pattern was held on Garland Sunday.[^5]

## Swimming the cattle and horses

At the lakeside and coastal gatherings, people drove their cattle and horses into the water to guard them against illness for the coming year.[^1] At Lough Keeran they threw in halters and spancels, along with lumps of butter, so the cows would give milk and butter through the year; the poor later gathered the butter from the water.[^1] In places boys mounted the horses and raced them through the lake, and on Galway Bay horses were ridden into the sea.[^1]

## Fairs

Lúnasa was a great season for fairs, and some still carry the old names, like Lammas Fair and Gooseberry Fair.[^1] The best-known survivor is the Puck Fair at Killorglin in Co. Kerry.[^1] These trace back in part to the ancient óenach assemblies, the Fair of Tailtiu and the Fair of Carman, both held at Lúnasa. The [origins article](/lunasa-pre-christian-origins) covers those assemblies in full.[^1]

## Garlands, cakes, bonfires, and weather

Smaller customs cluster around the same day. Girls made wild flowers into garlands and posies at the gatherings, the obvious guess for how Garland Sunday got its name.[^1] At the Grange stone circle by Lough Gur, the tallest stone (once called Rannach Chrom Dubh) was decked with flowers.[^1]

A festive cake was baked before the first of August, a barmbrack (bairín breac), the fruited bread still eaten in Ireland.[^1] At two Kerry gatherings the cake was set out for show, and the honour of cutting it went to a couple about to marry, or to the best dancers of the day.[^1]

Bonfires closed the hill gatherings in some districts, the same fires the young men raced round and leapt in the Slieve Bloom.[^1] And the day's divination was mostly weather-reading, as you'd expect at the harvest: watching for the "Lammas floods," or reading the far hills. In Limerick, when Knockfeerina looked blue and distant a fine harvest was coming; when it looked green and close at hand, the worst was feared.[^1]

## Crom Dubh's Sunday

Across Irish-speaking Ireland the festival Sunday had another name, Domhnach Crom Dubh, and it left a rich seam of accounts in the Schools' Collection.[^6] MacNeill, who catalogued the survivals, put the history plainly: "the festival was pagan, and it was transformed into a Christian celebration at certain sites."[^7]

Crom Dubh is worth an article of his own, so his legends and where he came from are covered in the [Crom Dubh article](/crom-dubh-lughnasadh). Here it's enough to say that his Sunday was the folk name for the harvest's opening day across much of the country, and that the name often outlasted the stories once told about him.

## Marking the day now

None of this needed money or special gear, which is a good part of why it lasted. You don't need any either.

Climb a local high spot and take in the view, the way people once climbed for the day. Pick and eat the first ripe fruit where you live, whatever's in season. Cook a meal built around the first thing your garden or your area gives up this time of year. If you grow anything, mark the first cutting rather than the date on the calendar, which is what the old rule against early harvesting was really about. The point of the day was to meet the harvest where you are, and that hasn't changed.
