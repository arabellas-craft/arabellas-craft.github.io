CR was born from a question that kept coming up in 1980s pagan circles: if you want to practice something actually rooted in Celtic tradition, where do you look? The answer, it turned out, required building a community to find it together.

The [first article in this series](/celtic-reconstructionism-introduction-guide) gives an overview of what CR is. This article covers where it came from.

## The Problem in the 1980s

The short version: Celtic deity names were being dropped into a Wiccan framework with no engagement with Celtic sources. Same ritual structure, same theology, same Wheel of the Year. You could attend a "Celtic" ritual and encounter a Lord and Lady, four quarters, and an athame, none of which have roots in pre-Christian Celtic practice.

Practitioners who wanted something historically grounded had nowhere obvious to go. Connection happened at gatherings and through correspondence. The 1985 Pagan Spirit Gathering in Wisconsin was a key early meeting point. Celtic discussions and workshops there drew people who went home and kept working, often in contact with each other.

Margot Adler's *Drawing Down the Moon* (1979) was widely read in these circles. Adler surveyed the pagan revival with genuine scholarly care, and the book was appreciated. It also clarified the gap. Adler documented the pagan landscape as it existed, and serious engagement with Celtic historical sources was largely absent from that landscape. CR emerged partly from asking what a practice built on those sources would actually look like.

## Online, Through the 1990s
The pipeline ran through a series of online spaces. PODS:CELTIC Echo on FidoNet BBS was an early one, where practitioners reconnected and started working through what a source-based Celtic practice would look like. That led to Nemeton-L, an email list founded by Erynn Rowan Laurie, a poet and Celtic studies scholar who became one of CR's central voices. From there, IMBAS. Then CompuServe, and eventually LiveJournal's [CR_R community](https://cr-r.livejournal.com/).

C. Lee Vermeers, one of the CR FAQ's co-authors, was part of those early PODS:CELTIC discussions. He describes the era as one of constantly pushing back against people who claimed "the Seekrit Celtic Wisdumb of the Ages, given to them by their grannies in a direct line of transmission from the Misty Mists of Misty Histry," <cite>[source](https://loraobrien.ie/celtic-paganism/)</cite> and occasionally getting space, between those interruptions, to discuss archaeology, folklore, and real history. The key documents of CR were collaboratively developed in these spaces, refined through years of argument and shared research.

## The Name: 1992

In the Spring 1992 issue of *Harvest* magazine (Southboro, Massachusetts), **Kym Lambert ní Dhoireann** used "Celtic Reconstructionist" as a tradition name for the first time in print. Ní Dhoireann credits **Kathryn Price NicDhàna** with coining the term. NicDhàna traces it to Adler's phrase "Pagan Reconstructionists" in *Drawing Down the Moon*. She applied that category specifically to Celtic practice. Both NicDhàna and ní Dhoireann have said they coined it to distinguish their practice from Wicca and eclectic neo-paganism.


## The Texts

Erynn Rowan Laurie's *A Circle of Stones* (1995, revised 2012) was the first book written by a CR practitioner for CR practitioners. Laurie's preface to the 2012 edition is worth reading on its own: she's frank that new archaeology and scholarship have shifted her thinking since 1995, and that much of what she asserted then isn't what she thinks now. That kind of honesty about revision is very on-brand for CR.

Alexei Kondratiev's *The Apple Branch* (1998, revised 2003) covers Celtic tradition across multiple cultures and appears on most CR reading lists. Kondratiev was writing before CR's methodology had solidified, and it shows in places (like Wiccan-influenced ritual structures). Read it knowing that context.

The *[CR FAQ](https://www.paganachd.com/faq/index.html)* is the movement's foundational document which be briefly talked about in [the previous article](/celtic-reconstructionism-introduction-guide). Authored collectively by NicDhàna, Laurie, C. Lee Vermeers, and ní Dhoireann, published online around 2006.

## The Organizations

**IMBAS** was a nonprofit that published *An Tríbhís Mhór: The IMBAS Journal of Celtic Reconstructionism* and the peer-reviewed *Air n-Aithesc*. It's now dormant.

**Gaol Naofa** ("Sacred Kinship," pronounced gweel NEE-fah) was founded in 2007 with a focus specifically on Irish, Scottish, and Manx Gaelic traditions. Gaol Naofa uses the term "Gaelic Polytheist" rather than "Celtic Reconstructionist." They understand Gaelic Polytheism as a subset of CR with a narrower geographic and linguistic scope. Their FAQ and articles are at [gaolnaofa.org](https://www.gaolnaofa.org).

**CAORANN** (Celts Against Oppression, Racism and Neo-Nazism) was founded to counter attempts at co-opting Celtic symbols by white nationalists.

## Where Things Stand

The formal infrastructure CR built in the 1990s and 2000s has mostly gone quiet. IMBAS is inactive. The mailing lists and LiveJournal communities are gone or dormant. [r/Paganacht](https://www.reddit.com/r/Paganacht/) carries a lot of active CR conversation today, as does the [Celtic Paganism](https://www.facebook.com/groups/celtic.paganism) Facebook group, co-administered by CR FAQ co-author C. Lee Vermeers.

CR as a formal movement peaked in the 1990s and 2000s, but CR as a methodology for taking Celtic tradition seriously is ongoing. The scholarship and source guides that came out of those decades are still available and still the foundation of the practice.
